=== QUANTUM MECHANICS NOTES ===

[1] INTRODUCTION
Quantum Mechanics is a branch of physics that studies the behavior of matter and energy at atomic and subatomic scales. It differs from classical physics by introducing probabilistic outcomes instead of deterministic ones.

Key idea:
At microscopic scales, particles do not have definite positions and velocities simultaneously.

---

[2] WAVE-PARTICLE DUALITY
Particles like electrons and photons exhibit both wave-like and particle-like properties.

Examples:
- Light behaves as a wave (interference, diffraction)
- Light behaves as particles (photoelectric effect)

De Broglie Hypothesis:
Every particle has a wavelength:
lambda = h / p
where h is Planck’s constant and p is momentum.

---

[3] HEISENBERG UNCERTAINTY PRINCIPLE
It is impossible to simultaneously know both the exact position and momentum of a particle.

Formula:
Δx * Δp ≥ ħ / 2

Meaning:
The more precisely you measure position, the less precisely you know momentum.

---

[4] SCHRÖDINGER EQUATION
The Schrödinger equation describes how the quantum state of a system evolves over time.

Time-dependent form:
iħ ∂ψ/∂t = Hψ

Where:
ψ = wave function
H = Hamiltonian operator

---

[5] WAVE FUNCTION (ψ)
The wave function contains all information about a quantum system.

|ψ|² gives probability density.

Interpretation:
- Not deterministic
- Gives likelihood of finding a particle at a position

---

[6] QUANTIZATION
Certain physical quantities take discrete values.

Examples:
- Energy levels in atoms
- Angular momentum

Electron energy in atom:
Eₙ = -13.6 / n² eV

---

[7] SUPERPOSITION
A quantum system can exist in multiple states at the same time until measured.

Example:
An electron can be in multiple positions simultaneously.

---

[8] ENTANGLEMENT
Two particles become linked such that the state of one instantly affects the other, regardless of distance.

This phenomenon challenges classical ideas of locality.

---

[9] MEASUREMENT PROBLEM
Observation causes the wave function to collapse into a definite state.

Before measurement:
System exists in superposition

After measurement:
Single outcome observed

---

[10] QUANTUM TUNNELING
Particles can pass through potential barriers even if they do not have enough energy classically.

Applications:
- Nuclear fusion
- Semiconductor devices

---

[11] OPERATORS IN QUANTUM MECHANICS
Physical observables are represented by operators.

Examples:
- Position operator: x
- Momentum operator: -iħ d/dx

---

[12] HAMILTONIAN
The Hamiltonian represents total energy (kinetic + potential).

It is used in Schrödinger equation.

---

[13] EIGENVALUES AND EIGENSTATES
Solving quantum equations gives eigenvalues (measurable quantities) and eigenstates (states of system).

Example:
Energy levels in atoms

---

[14] PAULI EXCLUSION PRINCIPLE
No two identical fermions can occupy the same quantum state simultaneously.

Important for:
- Atomic structure
- Periodic table

---

[15] QUANTUM NUMBERS
Used to describe electron states:

- n (principal quantum number)
- l (orbital angular momentum)
- m (magnetic quantum number)
- s (spin)

---

[16] SPIN
Intrinsic property of particles.

Electrons have spin = 1/2

---

[17] APPLICATIONS OF QUANTUM MECHANICS
- Semiconductors
- Lasers
- MRI
- Quantum computing
